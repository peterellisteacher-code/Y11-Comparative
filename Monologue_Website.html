Foundation Overview
The Five Foundations of a Monologue
Every strong monologue rests on five foundations. Let's meet them.
1
The Listener
Every monologue is spoken to someone — a person, an object, the audience, or oneself.
2
The Need to Speak
Why now? What forces this character to break their silence in this moment?
3
The Journey
A monologue takes us from Point A to Point B. Status, emotion, or sentence structure shifts.
4
The Voice
How does this character speak? Pace, vocabulary, sentence length, punctuation.
5
The Mini-Play
A monologue has a hook, a climax or turn, and a resolution — like a tiny one-act play.

Conflict Radar · 1 of 4
Internal vs external conflict
Every monologue is powered by conflict. Before you can write one, you need to be able to spot the two kinds.
Internal conflict

A struggle inside the character. Two desires, two fears, two versions of themselves — fighting each other.

Nora knows she must leave Torvald. She also can't bear to leave her children. That's internal.

External conflict

A struggle between the character and something outside them — a person, a rule, a situation, a society.

The Woman can't get to the tuna cans because a stranger is blocking her. That's external.

A rule of thumb
Most strong monologues have both. The external conflict is usually what triggers the speech — something has just happened. The internal conflict is what gives it weight — the speaker is fighting themselves as they speak.

Voice Lab · 1 of 6
The three monologue forms
Monologues don't all sound alike. The same character, in the same moment, can feel completely different depending on who they're speaking to. That's the form. Three to know.

1. Stream of consciousness

The character is speaking — but not to anyone in particular. We are overhearing their thoughts as they happen, in real time, before the mind has sorted them into neat sentences.

The listener is essentially no one: the character themselves, the inside of their own head.

Signals: run-on sentences · self-correction · sudden jumps · half-finished phrases · dashes instead of full stops

2. Direct address to audience

The character breaks the fourth wall and speaks straight to us — the people watching. We become their confidants, their witnesses, or sometimes their jury.

The listener is us: intimate, knowing, trusted.

Signals: "you" used to mean the audience · questions we're invited to answer · the character performing a version of themselves for us

3. Direct address to another character/s

The character speaks to someone on stage with them — but the other character barely responds (or doesn't get to). One voice dominates. This is still a monologue, not a dialogue.

The listener is a specific person or group present in the scene — which shapes what the speaker can say, hide, or risk.

Signals: names are used · the listener's presence shapes every line · sometimes silence from the listener is louder than the words
Why does the form matter?
The same information sounds completely different depending on who's hearing it. Saying "I'm not OK" to yourself, to a crowd, or to your mother are three different monologues. Across the next four screens you'll see examples of each — and then classify the Watch clips yourselves.
