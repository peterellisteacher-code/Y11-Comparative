Hannah’s Words

Four scenes in the order Hannah lives them — ages 6, 8, and 12. Fill the blanks with the exact word Hannah (or Louise) used.

Scene 1 of 4
Play age 6

Louise: Stick ’em up! Are you the WORD ▾ in this here town? These are my tickle guns, and I’m gonna getcha!
Hannah: (giggling) No!
Louise: You want me to chase you? You better WORD ▾!

Sheriff / Run — At six, Hannah is copying her mother’s vocabulary. “Sheriff” shows she is taking the invented world seriously, and “run” shows she has understood the rules of the game: the sheriff gives chase. The word choice tells us she is a child who trusts her mother’s imagination enough to live inside it.
The Drawing age 8

Hannah: Want to see my WORD ▾ for Miss Garriott’s class?
Louise: Yeah, little nose.
Hannah: We had to make up our own TV show. If we had one. That’s you and Dad. The show is called “Mommy and Daddy Talk to WORD ▾.”
Louise: Oh! Well, it’s lovely.

Project / Animals — Hannah is eight, her parents have separated, and the drawing reunites them on paper. “Project” frames her effort as serious work, the kind of word she has heard adults use. “Animals” is the word she chooses for the heptapods, and it is a hopeful one: it turns her mother’s strange past into something shared, gentle, and meaningful to both parents.
The Lakeside age 8

Hannah: He doesn’t look at me the same way anymore.
Louise: It’s my fault. I told him something that he wasn’t ready to hear.
Hannah: What? What’s going to happen?
Louise: It has to do with a really rare disease. And it’s WORD ▾. Kind of like you are, with your swimming, and your WORD ▾, and all the other amazing things that you share with the world.
Hannah: I’m unstoppable?
Louise: Yeah.

Unstoppable / Poetry — Louise is telling Hannah she is going to die, and her word choice is doing enormous protective work. “Unstoppable” is a word she can turn into a compliment once the diagnosis has been delivered. The placement of the diagnosis between “swimming” and “poetry” is deliberate: Louise is making sure the words around the bad news are the things that make Hannah’s life full.
The Homework age 12

Hannah: What’s the term for when you make a deal, and we both get something out of it?
Louise: A compromise.
Hannah: No… Like, it’s a competition. But both sides end up happy.
Louise: Like a win-win?
Hannah: More WORD ▾ than that.
Louise: If you want science, call your WORD ▾.

Science-y / Father — Hannah is twelve, and her word choice still carries a child’s suffix. “Science-y” is the word of someone who is almost but not quite a teenager. Louise’s reply, “call your father”, is a small but important detail. She does not say “try” or “if you can”; she assumes Ian is reachable, which tells us he and Louise are back in each other’s lives even if the film never shows how.
The four moments — for reference as you write:
  1. age 6: “Are you the sheriff? / You better run!”
  2. age 8: “My project for Miss Garriott’s class … Mommy and Daddy Talk to Animals.”
  3. age 8: “A really rare disease. And it’s unstoppable. Kind of like you are, with your swimming, and your poetry … I’m unstoppable?”
  4. age 12: “More science-y than that. / If you want science, call your father.”
