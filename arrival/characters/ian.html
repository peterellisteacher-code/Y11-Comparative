Ian’s Words

Ian believes science is the cornerstone of civilisation, at least until a linguist proves him wrong. Below are five moments from the film. For each, fill in his exact words.

First Meeting

Ian: Yeah, it’s great. Even if it’s ADJ ▾.
Louise: It’s ADJ ▾?
Ian: Well, the cornerstone of civilization isn’t NOUN ▾, it’s NOUN ▾.

Wrong / Wrong / Language / Science — This is Ian’s introduction, and the physicist here will spend the whole film disproving his own thesis. The dry “even if it’s wrong” is Ian humour at first sight, and the word “wrong” appearing twice in the same beat tells us Louise has caught the provocation and Ian has doubled down. By the end of the film, science has lost the argument to language.
Naming the Unknown

Louise: So, what are we gonna call them?
Ian: I don’t know. I was thinking NAME ▾ and NAME ▾.

Abbott / Costello — Ian reaches for a pop-culture comedy duo at the most extraordinary moment in human history. Abbott and Costello were famous for their “Who’s on First?” sketch, which is a routine built entirely on two people failing to communicate. Ian has named the aliens after the very thing Louise is trying to overcome, so the joke is also a summary of what the film is about.
Still Single

Ian: As I watch you steer us around these NOUN ▾ traps that I didn’t even know existed, it’s like, “what?” I guess that’s why I’m ADJ ▾.

Communication / Single — Ian compliments Louise and then lands on himself. “Communication traps” is a physicist’s framing of what a linguist does instinctively, and “still single” is the first crack in the scientist’s armour. The self-deprecation is doing real work here: in one sentence Ian is both admiring Louise and admitting something about himself.
What Would You Change?

Louise: If you could see your whole life from start to finish, would you change things?
Ian: ADV ▾ I’d say what I VERB ▾ more often. I-I don’t know.

Maybe / Felt — Ian is a physicist who has been asked a philosopher’s question, and he answers with an emotional one. “Maybe” is his hedge: he is a man who prefers certainty, but here he is honest about not having it. The past tense “felt” is the real tell, because Ian is already regretting silences that haven’t happened yet. The scientist is beginning to think the way Louise thinks.
It Was Meeting You

Ian: You know I’ve had my head tilted up to the NOUN ▾ for as long as I can VERB ▾. You know what VERB ▾ me the most? It wasn’t meeting PRN ▾. It was meeting PRN ▾.

Stars / Remember / Surprised / Them / You — This line completes Ian’s arc. The man who once said science was the cornerstone of civilisation now admits that the most important discovery of his life was not the aliens, it was Louise. “Stars” and “remember” place him as the lifelong scientist, and the pair “them” and “you” complete the inversion: them is what he came for and you is what he found.

  5. “You know I’ve had my head tilted up to the stars for as long as I can remember. You know what surprised me the most? It wasn’t meeting them. It was meeting you.”
